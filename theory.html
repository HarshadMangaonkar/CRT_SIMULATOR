Introduction

A Cathode Ray Tube (CRT) oscilloscope is an electronic device used to visualize electrical signals in the form of waveforms. It works by producing a focused beam of electrons inside a vacuum tube and displaying their impact on a fluorescent screen.

CRT technology was widely used in televisions, monitors, and measuring instruments. In an oscilloscope, it helps students observe voltage changes over time by converting electrical signals into visible patterns.

Main Parts of CRT

Heater: Heats the cathode to release electrons.

Cathode: Emits electrons through thermionic emission.

Control Grid: Controls the intensity and brightness of the beam.

Accelerating Anodes: Increase the speed of electrons.

Focusing Anode: Narrows the electron beam into a sharp line.

Deflection Plates: Control the direction of the beam.

Fluorescent Screen: Produces light when struck by electrons.

Working Principle

When power is supplied, the heater warms the cathode which emits electrons. These electrons are accelerated and focused into a narrow beam by the anodes.

The beam then passes through deflection plates. Electrical signals applied to these plates change the direction of the beam.

Finally, the electron beam strikes the phosphor-coated screen and produces visible light, creating a waveform.

Conclusion

The CRT oscilloscope converts electrical signals into visible waveforms using electron beam technology.
